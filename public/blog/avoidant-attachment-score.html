The Avoidant Attachment Score: Why You Pull Away and What It's Protecting

SIGNATUREWITHIN · 8 min read

There is a moment in relationships that avoidants know well. Things are going well — genuinely well — and then something shifts. A conversation gets too close. Someone needs too much. The intimacy that felt good starts to feel like pressure, and the instinct is to create distance. Not dramatically. Just enough space to breathe.

Avoidant attachment is the hardest pattern to self-identify — because the logic that creates distance also creates a convincing story about why that distance is reasonable. Understanding what the pattern is actually protecting is where the work begins.

What the score is measuring

  • Avoidant attachment develops in early environments where emotional needs were consistently unmet, minimized, or treated as burdensome
  • The strategy that emerges is self-sufficiency as protection — if I don't need, I can't be disappointed
  • Avoidants score low on need for intimacy and high on comfort with independence — but research shows this is a learned preference, not an innate one
  • Under stress, avoidants increase distance — the opposite of anxious attachers, who increase pursuit
  • They often genuinely believe they don't need closeness — until the relationship ends and the loss reveals the need they were suppressing

What the research reveals

Avoidant attachment is not the absence of need. It is need that has learned to hide itself so well that even the person carrying it stops recognizing it.
  • Avoidants tend to have rich internal worlds and thin external emotional expression — the feeling is there, the outlet has been closed
  • They often choose partners who pursue — which triggers the withdrawal cycle — or partners who are equally avoidant, which creates connection without real intimacy
  • The most reliable trigger for avoidant shutdown is the perception of being controlled or engulfed
  • Physical closeness is often easier than emotional closeness for this attachment style

The illustration that lands

The therapist and researcher John Bowlby, who developed attachment theory, spent years documenting what happens when children's bids for connection are consistently unmet. The avoidant pattern is not pathology — it is adaptation. A child who reaches and is not met learns to stop reaching. That is a survival strategy. It becomes a problem in adult relationships when the reaching would be safe — and the pattern keeps happening anyway.

What changes

The avoidant who understands their score stops interpreting their need for space as proof they don't want connection. They start getting curious about the specific moments when closeness triggers the pull-back — and what that pull-back is actually protecting.

Distance is a strategy, not a personality.
