Your Conflict Style Score: The Pattern You Repeat in Every Hard Conversation

SIGNATUREWITHIN · 8 min read

Think about the last real conflict you had with someone you care about. Not the surface argument — the thing underneath it. How you handled it. Whether you pushed in or pulled back. Whether it got resolved or got buried. Whether you said the thing you needed to say or whether you said what felt safe.

Your conflict style was mostly learned before you were old enough to choose it. It came from watching how the adults around you handled disagreement — and from the strategies you developed to survive the ones that didn't go well.

What the score is measuring

  • Research identifies four primary conflict styles: competing (high assertiveness, low cooperation), accommodating (low assertiveness, high cooperation), avoiding (low on both), and collaborating (high on both)
  • Most people have a default style they return to under stress, regardless of what their intellectual preference is
  • Conflict style is more predictive of relationship satisfaction than communication style — it's not how you talk normally, it's how you talk when it's hard
  • Avoiding is the most common default — and the most damaging over time, because it creates accumulation without resolution
  • The most relationally mature version of any style involves flexibility — the ability to read the situation and choose the approach, not just execute the default

What the research reveals

The conflict style that protected you in your family of origin is not always the one that serves you in adult relationships. But it is almost always the one that shows up first.
  • Competing styles often develop in environments where showing vulnerability was dangerous
  • Accommodating styles often develop where conflict was treated as abandonment risk
  • Avoiding styles often develop where conflict was unpredictable or explosive
  • Collaborating styles are not natural — they are almost always consciously developed

The illustration that lands

The research on divorce prediction, largely developed by John Gottman, consistently identifies four conflict patterns as the most destructive: criticism, contempt, defensiveness, and stonewalling. Three of these — contempt, defensiveness, and stonewalling — are versions of default conflict styles under stress. They are not character flaws. They are conflict styles that escalated beyond their useful range.

What changes

Understanding your conflict style score means you stop treating your default as the only available response. You start developing the capacity to choose — which is what the most relationally effective people have that others don't.

The pattern in conflict is the pattern in the relationship.
