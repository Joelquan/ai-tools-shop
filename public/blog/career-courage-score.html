Low Career Courage Score: The Gap Between What You Want and What You Actually Pursue

SIGNATUREWITHIN · 8 min read

There is a version of the career you wanted that you didn't pursue. Not because you couldn't. Because something stopped you before you got to the attempt. The fear of failure. The cost of disruption. The risk of what people would think. The question of whether you deserved it.

Career Courage is the dimension most correlated with career regret. Not skills, not intelligence, not opportunity. The willingness to pursue what you actually want despite the presence of fear.

What the score is measuring

  • Career Courage measures the gap between what someone values in work and what they are actually willing to pursue
  • Low scores are rarely about lack of ambition — they are about ambition that has been filtered through fear before it reaches action
  • Research on regret consistently shows that people regret inactions more than actions — the things they didn't try more than the things that didn't work
  • The most common low-courage pattern is serial preparation — acquiring more credentials, more experience, more readiness before making a move that fear is actually blocking
  • Career courage is context-dependent — the same person who is bold in one domain can be risk-averse in another

What the research reveals

Most people are not held back by lack of ability. They are held back by a risk calculation that overweights the cost of failure and underweights the cost of never trying.
  • The fear underlying low career courage is almost always one of three things: fear of failure, fear of judgment, or fear of success
  • External permission-seeking is the most visible symptom — waiting for someone to tell you it is okay to want what you want
  • The careers most often described as "safe choices" in retrospect were the riskiest — they cost the person the possibility of doing what they actually came here to do
  • The single most effective intervention for low career courage is finding evidence that the fear is not proportional to the actual risk

The illustration that lands

Research on deathbed regrets, conducted by palliative care nurses and documented in studies across multiple countries, consistently produces the same findings. The most common regret is not having lived a life true to oneself rather than the life others expected. The second most common is working too hard at the expense of relationships. Low career courage is the mechanism that produces the first regret.

What changes

A low Career Courage score is not a character verdict. It is information about where fear is currently making your career decisions for you — and an invitation to examine whether the fear is proportional to the actual risk.
