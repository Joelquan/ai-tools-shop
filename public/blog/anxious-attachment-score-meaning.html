What a High Anxious Attachment Score Means — and How to Work With It

SIGNATUREWITHIN·9 min read·Updated May 2026

In the 1950s, psychologist Harry Harlow conducted one of the most revealing — and most disturbing — experiments in the history of developmental psychology. He separated infant rhesus monkeys from their mothers and placed them with two surrogate "mothers": one made of wire that provided milk, and one made of cloth that provided nothing but warmth and softness. The conventional wisdom at the time predicted the infants would attach to the milk source. They did not. When frightened, when uncertain, when distressed — they ran overwhelmingly to the cloth mother. Not because she fed them. Because she was soft, and she was there.

Harlow's experiments established something that reshaped developmental psychology: the primary need of an infant is not food. It is a secure base — a reliable, warm presence that signals the world is safe and someone is available. When that signal is consistent, the infant develops what researchers call secure attachment. When it is inconsistent — sometimes present, sometimes withdrawn — the infant develops a different strategy: hypervigilance. Stay attuned to the caregiver's emotional state at all times. Amplify distress signals to ensure connection is maintained. Never fully relax.

Anxious attachment is not a personality flaw. It is an intelligent adaptation to an inconsistent early environment. The nervous system learned what it needed to learn to stay connected. The tragedy is that the strategy which worked at age two becomes the pattern that creates distance at age thirty-two.

What Your Score Is Measuring

The Research on Anxious Attachment
  • Anxious attachment affects approximately 20% of the adult population — the most common insecure attachment style
  • It develops primarily through inconsistent caregiving — not absent, but unpredictable: sometimes warm and responsive, sometimes withdrawn or preoccupied
  • Adults with anxious attachment show hyperactivated stress responses to perceived relationship threats — the amygdala fires as if physical danger is present
  • Research on relationship satisfaction shows anxiously attached individuals report significantly lower satisfaction even in objectively secure relationships
  • The most consistent predictor of anxious attachment severity in adulthood is not the presence of trauma but the unpredictability of early caregiving

How It Shows Up in Your Relationships

Most people who score high Anxious Attachment recognize these patterns immediately — they have been living them, often without having language for them:

  • Reading silence, response time, or tone as evidence of a problem — before any evidence exists
  • Feeling a strong need for reassurance — and finding that reassurance only lasts briefly before the anxiety returns
  • Escalating emotional expression when disconnection is perceived — to restore closeness faster
  • A persistent background monitoring of how the relationship is going, even during genuinely calm periods
  • Difficulty being the one who creates distance, even temporarily, even healthily
The Anxious-Avoidant Dynamic

In one of the most documented relationship patterns in attachment research, anxiously attached individuals disproportionately partner with avoidantly attached ones. The anxious partner moves toward connection under stress. The avoidant moves away. The more the anxious partner pursues, the more the avoidant withdraws. The more the avoidant withdraws, the more anxious the anxious partner becomes. Both are following their attachment programming. Neither is the villain. Both are recreating the original dynamic — the inconsistent caregiver — that shaped their nervous system. Recognizing this is not an accusation. It is a map.

What Actually Changes It

Attachment styles are not fixed destinies. The research is consistent: they are highly stable but genuinely changeable through three mechanisms — secure relational experiences, deliberate therapeutic work, and self-understanding applied consistently over time.

Self-understanding is where this begins, because it is what you have most control over today. Specifically: learning to name the activation when it is happening — not afterward, but in the moment. My nervous system is reading this silence as threat. I do not yet know if the threat is real. That gap between the feeling and the story the feeling generates is where change lives. The feeling is always real. The story is often not. Separating them is the work.

You are not too much. You are a nervous system that learned to love loudly in an environment that was sometimes not listening. The attachment system that formed to protect connection can be recalibrated. Not quickly. Not without discomfort. But genuinely — and the relationships on the other side of that work are different in kind, not just in degree.
