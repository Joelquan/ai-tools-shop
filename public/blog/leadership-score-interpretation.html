How to Read Your Leadership Score: A Dimension-by-Dimension Guide

SIGNATUREWITHIN · 8 min read

A score of 68 in Vision means something very different from a score of 68 in Delegation. The number is the same. The implication is not. Reading a leadership profile requires understanding what each dimension is actually measuring — and what the interactions between dimensions tell you.

This article is a guide to your own scores. Not a generic description of leadership. A map of what your specific numbers mean for how you currently lead — and where the highest leverage for development actually is.

What the score is measuring

  • Vision measures your clarity about where you are taking your team or organization and your ability to communicate it compellingly
  • Execution measures your capacity to translate vision into specific, actionable plans and to hold yourself and others accountable to them
  • People Development measures how actively you invest in the growth of the people you lead
  • Delegation measures your ability to transfer responsibility appropriately rather than concentrating ownership
  • Resilience measures how you recover from setbacks, pressure, and failure — and how your recovery affects the people around you

What the research reveals

Leadership development is most efficient when it targets the specific dimension creating the most friction — not the dimension that is easiest to work on.
  • High Vision with low Execution is the most common profile in founders and creative leaders — inspiring to follow, difficult to work for operationally
  • High Execution with low Vision creates managers who run excellent operations in search of a direction
  • Low Delegation is the most common ceiling for leaders who were high-performing individual contributors
  • Low People Development often reflects a leader who was never developed themselves and doesn't have a model for what it looks like
  • Resilience is the hardest dimension to develop because it requires actual adversity — you can only demonstrate it in conditions that require it

The illustration that lands

Research on military leadership — one of the most studied leadership contexts — consistently shows that technical competence is necessary but not sufficient. The leaders who sustain performance under pressure are the ones with high resilience scores and high people development scores. They lose fewer people to breakdown, burnout, and exit. The technical leaders often win the battle and lose the unit.

What changes

Your leadership profile is not a grade. It is a diagnosis. The dimension with the most room for development is almost always the one creating the most friction in your current role — and that is where to start.
